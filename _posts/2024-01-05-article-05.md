### A Different Kind of First Lecture

One of the most memorable early experiences in my university life came from the first lecture of our Programming Fundamentals course taught by Dr. Bilal Ahmad, a researcher and expert in Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning, known for connecting computer science fundamentals with real-world systems and industry-level thinking.

Like many first-semester students at UET Lahore Faisalabad Campus, I entered the classroom expecting a traditional introduction filled with course outlines, grading policies, and technical details. Since everything around me was new, I was already trying to adjust to university life, new classmates, new teachers, and a completely different learning environment.

To my surprise, the lecture did not begin with programming concepts or technical slides. Instead, Dr. Bilal Ahmad started with a casual conversation about student life, adaptation challenges, and the transition from school to university. His approach immediately created a comfortable atmosphere in the classroom. Rather than feeling nervous, I felt relaxed and ready to listen.

As a new Computer Engineering student, I was still trying to understand what university education would actually be like. Everything seemed unfamiliar. I had many questions about my future, my degree, and the skills I would need to develop. Listening to an AI and ML-oriented academic thinker speak openly about student life helped me realize that these feelings were normal.

That first lecture felt different because it focused on mindset before technical knowledge. Looking back now, I believe this approach was valuable because success in engineering is not only about learning concepts, but also about developing problem-solving thinking and a research-driven mindset.

The relaxed discussion also helped me adjust to university life. During my first few days on campus, everything felt unfamiliar. That lecture gave me confidence and reminded me that learning is a gradual process built on consistency and curiosity.

Before that first lecture, I had no real expectations about who would be teaching Programming Fundamentals. Like many students arriving at university for the first time, I assumed it would just be another classroom with another teacher going through slides.

What I did not expect was to find out that Dr. Bilal Ahmad had completed his graduate studies in China, specializing in Artificial Intelligence. He had worked on training Machine Learning models using real medical research data, with a primary focus on cancer detection and related healthcare applications. Hearing this in a first-semester classroom was genuinely surprising to me.

There was a mix of two feelings that I still remember clearly. The first was inspiration. Knowing that someone with that level of achievement and specialization was standing in front of us and teaching a foundational course felt meaningful. It made the subject feel more serious, more connected to something bigger than just passing an exam.

The second feeling was something closer to intimidation. I was a new student who had barely adjusted to university life, and here was an individual who had studied abroad, contributed to AI research in the medical field, and was now bringing that knowledge into a programming classroom at UET Faisalabad Campus. The gap between where I was and where he had reached felt enormous at that moment.

But as the lecture continued, that intimidation gradually turned into motivation. I realized that this was exactly the kind of environment I had entered university to be part of. The fact that someone with such deep expertise in Machine Learning and Deep Learning was teaching us foundational programming was not something to be nervous about. It was something to appreciate and take seriously.

### Understanding the Difference Between Labels and Skills

During that same lecture, Dr. Bilal Ahmad raised a question about PEC accreditation and NCEAC accreditation in Pakistan. Most students in the room, including myself, were not sure about the details.

He explained that NCEAC, the National Computing Education Accreditation Council, oversees computing-related programs such as Computer Science, Software Engineering, Artificial Intelligence, and similar disciplines. PEC, the Pakistan Engineering Council, traditionally handles engineering programs like Electrical, Mechanical, and Civil Engineering. Computer Engineering, by nature, sits at the intersection of both computing and engineering, which creates some ambiguity about which body it falls under.

At UET Lahore Faisalabad Campus, our Computer Engineering program was under NCEAC rather than PEC. For many students, this was new information. Some had assumed that anything with the word "engineering" in its name would automatically fall under PEC.

What made the conversation memorable was not just the clarification itself, but where Dr. Bilal Ahmad took it next. He pointed out that students often spend significant energy worrying about the label of their degree, which council accredits it, and how it compares to programs at other universities. While these are not unimportant questions, he brought the focus back to something more fundamental.

> "In the real world, people do not ask about the label of your degree. They ask what you can build."

At that moment, the discussion shifted from academic structure to professional reality.

I began to understand that while degrees provide structure and credibility, real-world impact comes from applied skills, problem-solving ability, and practical execution. A student who deeply understands programming logic, data structures, and algorithmic thinking will always have more to offer than one who focused only on the name of their qualification.

This changed how I viewed Programming Fundamentals. Instead of seeing it as a subject to pass, I started seeing it as a tool for building systems and solving real problems.

### How That Lesson Shaped My First Semester

As the semester continued, the message about skills over labels became increasingly relevant.

Whenever I faced difficulties in programming concepts like loops, functions, or logic building, I reminded myself that the goal was not just to complete assignments but to build understanding.

Mistakes became part of the learning process rather than failures.

Slowly, I began to see programming as a foundation for solving real-world problems rather than just an academic requirement. Dr. Bilal Ahmad's own work made this connection feel tangible. When someone who has trained Machine Learning models on medical datasets and worked on cancer research tells you to focus on what you can build, the advice carries a weight that is different from a generic motivational statement. It is coming from someone who has actually done it.

During later lectures, Dr. Bilal Ahmad also introduced broader ideas from Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning, emphasizing how modern systems depend heavily on data-driven decision-making and the practical application of algorithms. These discussions helped bridge the gap between the fundamental concepts we were learning and the advanced technologies shaping today's world.

Many of the research areas he discussed in class are reflected in his published academic work. His research publications, citations, and scholarly contributions can be explored through his **[Google Scholar profile](https://scholar.google.com.au/citations?user=8nZ0jVkAAAAJ&hl=en)**.

This mindset also influenced how I approached my Computer Engineering journey. I realized that university is not only about earning a degree, but about building technical and analytical abilities that remain useful beyond academics.

This is also one of the reasons I created this GitHub portfolio blog — to document my learning journey and reflect on my growth as a Computer Engineering student.

Looking back, the most important lesson from that lecture was not technical content, but a shift in thinking.

Skills matter more than labels, and consistent learning matters more than comparison.

That mindset continues to guide me throughout my academic journey in Computer Engineering.
