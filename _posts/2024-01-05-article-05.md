# A Different Kind of First Lecture

One of the most memorable early experiences in my university life came from the first lecture of our Programming Fundamentals course taught by Dr. Bilal Ahmad. Like many first-semester students at UET Lahore Faisalabad Campus, I entered the classroom expecting a traditional introduction filled with course outlines, grading policies, and technical details. Since everything around me was new, I was already trying to adjust to university life, new classmates, new teachers, and a completely different learning environment.

To my surprise, the lecture did not begin with programming concepts or technical slides. Instead, Dr. Bilal Ahmad started with a casual conversation. He talked about university life, hostel experiences, student challenges, and the adjustments that many students face when moving away from home for the first time. His approach immediately created a comfortable atmosphere in the classroom. Rather than feeling nervous, I felt relaxed and ready to listen.

As a new Computer Engineering student, I was still trying to understand what university education would actually be like. Everything seemed unfamiliar. I had many questions about my future, my degree, and the skills I would need to develop. Listening to an experienced professor speak openly about student life helped me realize that these feelings were normal. Many students around me appeared to be thinking about the same concerns.

That first lecture felt different because it focused on mindset before technical knowledge. Looking back now, I believe that this approach was valuable because success in engineering is not only about learning concepts. It is also about developing the right attitude toward learning, problem-solving, and continuous improvement. Before teaching us programming, Dr. Bilal Ahmad encouraged us to think about why we were studying and what we hoped to achieve in the future.

The relaxed discussion also helped me become more comfortable with university life. During my first few days on campus, I was still adjusting to a completely new environment. Everything felt unfamiliar, and I was unsure about what to expect from my studies. That lecture gave me confidence and reminded me that learning is a gradual process. Nobody starts university knowing everything. Growth comes through consistency, patience, and effort.

# Understanding the Difference Between Labels and Skills

During that same lecture, Dr. Bilal Ahmad asked a question that immediately captured everyone's attention. He asked whether we knew the difference between PEC accreditation and NCEAC accreditation in Pakistan. Many students, including myself, were unsure about the details. Although we had heard these terms before, very few of us fully understood their significance.

He explained that different academic programs are recognized through different accreditation systems. Some programs fall under engineering accreditation while others are associated with computing and technology accreditation bodies. For many students, this discussion was completely new. We listened carefully because it related directly to our academic future.

What made the conversation memorable was not simply the explanation itself. It was the conclusion that followed. After discussing accreditation systems and degree labels, Dr. Bilal Ahmad shared a statement that stayed with me long after the lecture ended:

> "In the real world, people do not ask about the label of your degree. They ask what you can build."

At that moment, the entire discussion suddenly felt much more meaningful. Until then, I had assumed that the title of a degree was one of the most important factors in determining success. Like many new students, I sometimes worried about comparisons between different academic programs and institutions. However, that statement shifted my perspective.

I began to understand that employers, organizations, and industries often place significant value on practical skills, problem-solving ability, and real contributions. A degree provides a foundation, but the ability to apply knowledge effectively is what truly creates opportunities. Instead of focusing on labels, students should focus on developing meaningful skills and gaining real experience.

This idea also changed how I viewed Programming Fundamentals. Initially, I saw programming as just another subject that I needed to pass. However, after hearing that discussion, I started to see programming as a practical tool. It was not simply about earning marks in examinations. It was about learning how to create solutions, automate tasks, and build useful systems.

As the semester progressed, this mindset continued to influence my approach to learning. Whenever I encountered a difficult topic, I reminded myself that the goal was not merely to memorize information. The goal was to understand concepts deeply enough to use them in practical situations. This perspective made learning more meaningful and helped me stay motivated during challenging weeks.

During several Programming Fundamentals lectures, Dr. Bilal Ahmad also shared insights about modern technology fields such as Artificial Intelligence (AI), Machine Learning (ML), and Deep Learning (DL). Although these topics were far beyond the scope of our introductory programming course, hearing about them sparked my curiosity. He often emphasized that technology should be used to solve real-world problems rather than simply demonstrate technical knowledge.

I also learned that high-quality data plays a critical role in building reliable intelligent systems. Dr. Bilal Ahmad frequently highlighted the importance of working with accurate and carefully collected datasets, particularly medical datasets, because precision and reliability are essential when technology affects human lives. These discussions helped me realize that even the simple programming concepts we were learning formed the foundation for much larger and more impactful technologies.

# How That Lesson Shaped My First Semester

As my first semester continued, I found myself thinking about that lecture more often than I expected. The message about skills over labels became relevant in many situations. Whether I was learning programming concepts, working on assignments, or preparing for quizzes, I gradually started focusing more on understanding and application rather than simply completing requirements.

Programming Fundamentals became one of the first subjects that challenged me to think differently. Since I had limited programming experience before joining university, many concepts initially seemed difficult. Variables, loops, functions, and logical problem-solving required a completely new way of thinking. There were moments when I felt confused and frustrated. Sometimes errors appeared in my programs for reasons I could not immediately understand.

However, the mindset introduced during that first lecture encouraged me to keep moving forward. Instead of treating mistakes as failures, I began viewing them as part of the learning process. Every error helped me discover something new. Every successful program increased my confidence. Slowly, concepts that once seemed complicated became easier to understand.

The lesson also influenced how I thought about my future as a Computer Engineering student. I realized that university is not only a place for earning a degree. It is a place for building knowledge, developing practical abilities, and preparing for real challenges. Skills grow through consistent effort, curiosity, and practice. They cannot be acquired instantly.

This understanding also aligns with one of the main reasons I created this GitHub portfolio blog. Inspired by the encouragement of Dr. Bilal Ahmad, I wanted to document my learning journey and leave a digital footprint as a Computer Engineering student. Each article represents a small step in my academic development. By reflecting on experiences, challenges, and lessons learned, I can track my growth while sharing my journey with others.

Looking back, it is remarkable that one of the most important lessons from my first semester did not involve a programming syntax rule or a technical formula. Instead, it came from a simple conversation about degrees, accreditation, and skills. The message was clear: labels may open doors, but abilities determine how far someone can go after those doors are opened.

That first lecture replaced uncertainty with motivation. It encouraged me to focus on learning, building, and improving rather than comparing labels or worrying about perceptions. As I continue my studies in Computer Engineering, I still carry that lesson with me. It serves as a reminder that meaningful progress comes from developing skills, solving problems, and continuously striving to become better than I was yesterday.
