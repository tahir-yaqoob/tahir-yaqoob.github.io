### Confusion During the First Weeks of University

The first few weeks of university were filled with excitement, curiosity, and a fair amount of confusion. As a first-semester BS Computer Engineering student at UET Lahore Faisalabad Campus, I was entering a completely new environment. Everything felt different from school and college. There were new classmates, new instructors, unfamiliar buildings, and a schedule that seemed much more complicated than anything I had experienced before.

Like many new students, I was trying to adjust to university life while also learning how to manage my responsibilities independently. In school, schedules were usually straightforward and teachers often reminded students about important activities. University was different. Students were expected to keep track of their own classes, laboratories, announcements, and deadlines.

At the beginning of the semester, our class was divided into two groups, G1 and G2, for laboratory sessions. I was assigned to Group 2. Since everything was still new, many students were still trying to understand which laboratories they were supposed to attend and when. There were multiple course schedules, different rooms, and several announcements that required attention.

During one of the early weeks of the semester, confusion about the laboratory schedule caused several students, including me, to make a mistake. We believed we were going to the correct laboratory session, but in reality we had misunderstood the schedule. At that moment, none of us realized that a simple misunderstanding would later become one of the most memorable lessons of my first semester.

Looking back now, I realize that those early weeks were not only about learning academic concepts. They were also about learning how to manage university life. Organization, responsibility, and attention to detail are skills that every student must develop. At the time, however, I was still learning these lessons.

### The Programming Fundamentals Lab We Were Not Supposed to Attend

On the day of the incident, several students from my group mistakenly attended the Programming Fundamentals laboratory session instead of our scheduled AICTE lab. Since many students were moving toward the same room, it seemed reasonable to follow along. We genuinely believed we were attending the correct session.

Ironically, the lab itself was extremely useful and interesting.

The session focused on setting up the tools we would use for Programming Fundamentals. During the lab, we learned how to install Visual Studio Code and configure the Python programming environment. Since Programming Fundamentals was one of the most important courses in our first semester, I was excited to learn about the software and tools that professional programmers use.

Before joining university, I had very limited experience with programming environments. Watching the instructor explain how Visual Studio Code works and how Python programs can be created and executed was fascinating. I carefully followed every step, making sure that the installation process worked correctly on my computer.

I still remember the feeling of satisfaction when everything was configured successfully. For a student who was just beginning to learn programming, even small achievements felt meaningful. Running a simple Python program and seeing the expected output on the screen gave me confidence and motivation to continue learning.

Programming Fundamentals was taught by Dr. Bilal Ahmad, and during the early weeks of the semester I was gradually beginning to understand how important programming would be throughout my academic journey. Learning Python, understanding programming logic, and becoming comfortable with development tools were all important milestones.

The laboratory session itself went smoothly. I actively participated, followed instructions, and learned valuable information that would help me throughout the semester. Because of that, I left the room feeling positive about the experience.

Unfortunately, the realization came later.

After the session ended, we discovered that we had attended the wrong laboratory. Our actual scheduled session had been the AICTE lab, not the Programming Fundamentals lab. Because we were absent from the correct laboratory session, our attendance was marked accordingly. In addition, marks associated with that lab activity were deducted.

At first, the situation felt frustrating. From my perspective, I had attended a laboratory session, participated actively, and spent my time learning. It did not feel like I had skipped class. However, university procedures are based on official schedules and attendance records. Since we were not present in the laboratory assigned to our group, the attendance system could only record our absence.

That moment was disappointing, but it also became the beginning of an important lesson.

> One wrong room can teach you the importance of double-checking everything.

### Learning Responsibility Through a Simple Mistake

As frustrating as the experience felt at the time, it ultimately taught me something valuable. Instead of focusing only on the attendance deduction, I began thinking about what had actually caused the mistake. The answer was simple: I had relied on assumptions instead of verification.

Rather than checking the schedule carefully myself, I followed what others appeared to be doing. While this may seem like a minor issue, it showed me how easily mistakes can happen when important details are overlooked.

From that day onward, I became much more careful about schedules, announcements, and course instructions. Whenever a new notification appeared in a class group or on a university platform, I made sure to read it carefully. Before attending a laboratory session, I would verify the room number, timing, and group allocation. These habits helped me avoid similar problems during the rest of the semester.

The lesson extended beyond laboratory schedules. I realized that responsibility is not only about completing assignments or preparing for examinations. It also involves organization, planning, and attention to detail. Small mistakes can sometimes create larger consequences than expected.

Interestingly, this lesson has similarities to programming itself. In Programming Fundamentals, a tiny mistake in code can prevent an entire program from working correctly. A missing bracket, incorrect indentation, or misplaced character can create errors that stop a program from running. Programming teaches students to be careful and systematic. In a similar way, this laboratory incident taught me the importance of checking details before taking action.

As the semester progressed, I noticed that I became more independent because of this experience. Instead of depending entirely on others for information, I developed the habit of confirming things myself. Whether it involved laboratory schedules, assignment deadlines, classroom locations, or examination dates, I became much more proactive.

Looking back today, I no longer remember the incident as a problem. Instead, I remember it as a learning experience. While losing attendance and marks was disappointing, the lesson I gained was much more valuable in the long run. University education is not only about learning technical subjects. It is also about developing habits that help students become responsible and reliable individuals.

One of the reasons I created this GitHub portfolio blog is to document experiences like this. Academic growth is not only measured through grades and achievements. It is also reflected in the lessons learned from mistakes. Sometimes a small misunderstanding can teach a lesson that stays with us for years.While reflecting on experiences from my first semester, I also explored some of the educational content and student interactions shared through **[Dr. Bilal Ahmad's Facebook page](https://www.facebook.com/Dr.BilalAhm)**. Seeing how academic discussions continue beyond the classroom reminded me that learning is not limited to lectures and laboratory sessions.

The wrong laboratory session taught me to pay attention, verify information, and take responsibility for my decisions. Although it seemed like a minor incident at the time, it became one of the memorable experiences of my first semester. It reminded me that growth often comes from mistakes, and that even simple experiences can become valuable lessons when we take the time to reflect on them.
