When the admission process ended, I finally received an offer for Computer Engineering at the Faisalabad campus of UET Lahore. Although my original goal had been the Lahore main campus, I decided to accept the opportunity and continue my journey there.

Around that time, severe floods had affected many parts of Pakistan. Because of the situation, universities remained closed for nearly fifteen days. The campus was still not fully active when a few friends and I decided to travel to Faisalabad two days before the official hostel allotment date.

### First impression of the campus

It was my first visit to Faisalabad. Even the rickshaw driver did not know where the university was, and I had to guide him with Google Maps. That made the place feel even more unfamiliar.

When we arrived, the surroundings looked isolated and almost deserted. The campus seemed cut off from the main city. Because it had been closed for days due to floods and vacation, the roads were messy, grass was overgrown, and the environment felt more like an abandoned area than an active university.

> The overgrown campus and unfamiliar surroundings made me question my decision for a moment.

### Hostel worries and fee surprise

The first visit did not allow document submission, so we returned home and came back again on the official date. This time the campus felt alive. More students were present and offices were working normally.

During hostel allotment, another surprise appeared. We were told every student had to immediately submit a security fee of 12,000 PKR for the hostel mess, and we were told to pay it in cash on the spot. None of us had expected that.

I was with my friends Muhammad Zain and Ali Shirazi, and for a few minutes we thought we might lose our hostel rooms. Fortunately, my elder brother had enough cash with him and helped us complete the payment. Later we discovered the fee could also be paid online, but at that moment we had only been told about cash.

### The first real technical moment

In the first Programming Fundamentals lab, we installed Python and Visual Studio Code. For many students, that may sound like a very small task, but for me it felt like the real beginning of my engineering journey. Until then, university had mostly consisted of admissions, hostel arrangements, paperwork, and adjusting to a new environment. This was one of the first moments where I actually felt connected to the field I had chosen.

The lab was primarily introductory. We were not yet learning programming concepts in depth. Instead, the focus was on preparing our systems for future work. We installed Python, configured Visual Studio Code, and learned how to create and use a virtual environment. At the time, I did not fully understand why a virtual environment was important, but I quickly learned that setting it up was not always straightforward.

My computer created unexpected problems. Because of issues with the Python installation and VS Code configuration, I struggled to get everything working correctly. For a while, I thought I had made a mistake somewhere. Eventually, I switched to another computer and successfully completed the setup process. Once I understood the steps, the task suddenly became much easier.

What happened next was something I still remember clearly.

Several classmates were facing similar issues with their systems. Some did not have Python installed properly, while others were having trouble creating the virtual environment. Since I had already solved the problem myself, I started helping them one by one. Before I realized it, I had configured Python and virtual environments on several computers across the lab.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad encouraged this kind of collaboration. One thing I appreciated about his teaching approach was that he allowed students to help one another rather than treating every task as an individual competition. He believed that problem-solving and teamwork were important skills for engineers, and the laboratory environment reflected that philosophy.

During that session, he shared a statement that stayed in my memory:

> "Even if you ask seventh-semester seniors to set up a virtual environment, many of them won't be able to do it."

At the time, the comment sounded surprising. We were first-semester students who had only recently entered university, so it was difficult to imagine senior students struggling with something that seemed so basic. However, as time passed, I realized there was truth behind his statement. Technical skills fade when they are not practiced, and many students focus only on completing assignments rather than understanding the tools they use.

That introductory lab also reflected one of the teaching approaches that I would notice repeatedly throughout the semester. Dr. Bilal Ahmad encouraged students to help one another instead of struggling alone. Whenever someone solved a problem, he often motivated them to assist their classmates and share what they had learned. Looking back, that environment helped develop not only technical skills but also teamwork and problem-solving abilities. By the end of the lab, I had not only configured my own system but had also helped several classmates get their setups working correctly. Looking back, it was a small achievement, yet it gave me confidence. It was one of the first moments in university where I felt capable of solving a technical problem and helping others do the same.

The lab itself was simple. We only printed a basic "Hello World" program and completed the initial setup. Yet that session represented something much bigger than a few lines of code. It marked the point where Computer Engineering stopped feeling like a distant goal and started feeling like a practical journey.

Looking back now, that first Programming Fundamentals lab remains memorable not because of the code we wrote, but because of what it taught me. It showed me that engineering is not only about learning concepts. It is also about troubleshooting problems, helping others, sharing knowledge, and continuously learning new tools. In many ways, that small introductory lab reflected the mindset that would guide me throughout the rest of my university journey.
