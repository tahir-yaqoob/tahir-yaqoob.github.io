# Adjusting to a New Way of Learning

After returning home and settling into the online learning routine, I quickly realized that studying remotely was very different from attending physical classes. Although the university had successfully shifted its academic activities online, students still needed time to adapt to the new environment.

Fortunately, I had internet access at home, which allowed me to attend most lectures without major interruptions. However, like many students, I frequently faced connectivity problems. Sometimes the internet connection became unstable during important parts of a lecture. At other times, the audio would stop, the video would freeze, or the meeting would disconnect completely.

These interruptions often made learning frustrating. In a physical classroom, students can easily ask questions or request clarification when they miss something. Online learning was different. If the connection lagged for even a few seconds, important explanations could be missed entirely.

The challenge was not limited to me. Many of my classmates experienced similar difficulties. Students living in different cities and villages faced varying internet conditions, and not everyone had access to a stable connection. As a result, online education created new obstacles that had not existed in traditional classrooms.

Another major difference was the absence of daily interaction with classmates. During physical classes, students naturally discuss assignments, share notes, and help each other understand difficult topics. In the hostel, group study sessions had become a normal part of university life.

Online learning changed that completely.

Instead of sitting together in classrooms or study rooms, everyone was now attending lectures individually from their homes. Communication continued through messages and calls, but it was not the same as face-to-face discussions.

> Learning alone through a screen felt very different from learning together in a classroom.

This change made me appreciate how important collaboration and social interaction are in the learning process. Academic success is not only about listening to lectures; it is also about discussing ideas, asking questions, and learning from other students.

Despite these challenges, I remained committed to continuing my studies and gradually adjusted to the new reality of online education.

# Long Hours, Assignments, and Digital Fatigue

One challenge that became increasingly noticeable during online classes was the amount of time spent in front of a screen.

In a traditional classroom environment, students naturally move between lecture halls, laboratories, libraries, and hostel rooms. Online learning removed much of that movement. Instead, lectures, assignments, communication, and study sessions all took place through laptops and mobile devices.

As a result, many hours of each day were spent staring at a screen.

Initially, I did not think much about it. However, after several weeks of continuous online classes, I began noticing eye strain and fatigue. My eyes often felt tired after long lecture sessions, especially when multiple classes were scheduled on the same day.

At times, I became concerned about the effect that prolonged screen exposure might have on my eyesight. Fortunately, with proper rest and reduced screen usage outside academic activities, the discomfort gradually disappeared and my eyesight returned to normal.

This experience taught me the importance of balancing technology use with personal well-being. Online learning offers many advantages, but it also requires students to take care of their physical health by managing screen time responsibly.

Another challenge was the increasing number of assignments.

Because online teaching methods differed from traditional classroom instruction, many teachers assigned additional tasks to ensure that students remained engaged with course material. Assignments, reports, quizzes, and practice exercises became regular parts of the weekly schedule.

Managing these responsibilities required strong time management skills. Students were expected to attend online lectures while simultaneously completing coursework for multiple subjects. Some days felt particularly demanding because deadlines from different courses arrived around the same time.

Nevertheless, these experiences helped me develop greater discipline and organizational skills. I learned how to plan my work, prioritize important tasks, and maintain a consistent study schedule even when circumstances were unusual.

Looking back, the challenges of online education were teaching valuable lessons beyond the academic content itself.

# Learning New Digital Systems

Perhaps the most unexpected aspect of online education was the need to learn entirely new digital systems and platforms.

Before this period, most academic activities were conducted in classrooms, laboratories, or through printed materials. Suddenly, students were expected to participate in lectures, submit assignments, communicate with instructors, and access learning resources through various online platforms.

Each teacher seemed to have a preferred method for managing classes.

Some instructors used one platform, while others used something completely different. As a result, students often found themselves learning new software almost as frequently as they were learning course content.

One example that I remember clearly involved a teacher who conducted lectures through Google Calendar. At that time, many students, including me, had very little experience using Google Calendar for academic purposes. We were accustomed to joining meetings through direct links, but now we had to navigate calendar invitations and scheduled events before entering the lecture.

What may seem simple today felt surprisingly confusing at the time.

Many students spent their first few attempts simply figuring out how to access the lecture correctly. Technical issues and unfamiliar interfaces occasionally caused delays, adding another layer of complexity to online learning.

A similar situation occurred when one instructor asked us to submit an assignment through email. Today, sending an email attachment seems straightforward, but at that time many first-year students had limited experience with formal academic email submissions.

The assignment carried significant marks, nearly ten in total, making the situation particularly stressful.

Several students, including me, were unsure about the correct format, subject line, attachment process, and submission procedure. Nobody wanted to lose marks because of a technical mistake rather than an academic one.

Because of this confusion, students requested the class representative to discuss the issue with the instructor. Fortunately, the teacher understood the concerns and agreed to change the submission method.

The assignment was eventually moved to Google Classroom, which immediately simplified the process. Most students found Google Classroom easier to understand, and assignment submissions became much more organized.

This experience highlighted an important reality of modern education: digital literacy is now an essential skill. Students are expected not only to understand academic subjects but also to navigate a wide range of digital tools and platforms effectively.

As the month progressed, my classmates and I gradually became more comfortable with online learning systems. Tasks that initially seemed complicated eventually became routine. We learned how to join virtual lectures, submit assignments digitally, communicate with instructors online, and manage our academic responsibilities through technology.

Although the transition was challenging, it ultimately helped us develop valuable technical skills that continue to be useful today.

Looking back, the online learning period was one of the most unusual phases of my university journey. It tested our adaptability, patience, and willingness to learn new systems. While the experience was not always easy, it demonstrated how students and educational institutions can continue moving forward even during unexpected circumstances.
