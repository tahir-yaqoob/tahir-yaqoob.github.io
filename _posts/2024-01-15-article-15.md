The transition from physical classes to online learning was one of the most unusual experiences of my academic journey. Until that point, university life had revolved around classrooms, laboratories, discussions with friends, and daily interactions with teachers. Suddenly, everything shifted to screens, internet connections, and virtual meetings.

Like many students, I initially believed that online classes might be easier than attending university physically. However, it did not take long to realize that online learning came with its own challenges. Although I was fortunate to have internet access at home, staying connected consistently was not always easy.

What appeared simple on paper often became frustrating in practice.

## Adjusting to a New Academic Environment

Most of our classes were conducted through Google Meet, although some instructors occasionally used Zoom. At first, many students were unfamiliar with these platforms. Joining lectures, finding meeting links, managing assignments, and navigating different online systems became part of our daily routine.

A typical day involved waking up, joining classes from my laptop, taking screenshots of important slides, and trying to remain focused for long periods in front of a screen. Unlike physical classrooms, online learning required a greater level of self-discipline. There was no teacher physically present to ensure students remained attentive.

One challenge I frequently faced involved my internet device. My family's electricity system switched between solar power and the regular WAPDA supply depending on availability. Whenever that transition occurred, my internet device would briefly shut down and restart.

Although the interruption often lasted only a minute or two, it was enough to disconnect me from an ongoing lecture.

Many times I would be listening carefully to an explanation, only to suddenly lose connection. By the time I rejoined the meeting, I had already missed an important concept or example. These small interruptions accumulated over time and made online learning more difficult than I had expected.

> A two-minute disconnection often felt much longer when it happened during an important explanation.

To compensate, I developed a habit of taking screenshots during lectures. This proved especially useful in subjects where instructors did not immediately provide notes after class. I would review the screenshots later and use them to prepare for quizzes and examinations.

The experience also taught me how dependent modern education had become on technology. In a traditional classroom, a brief power interruption might not affect learning significantly. During online education, however, even a short internet disruption could mean missing an entire explanation, class discussion, or important announcement.

## Dr. Bilal's Dedication During Online Classes

Among all the instructors teaching during that semester, Dr. Bilal Ahmad stood out because of his commitment to teaching. Even after moving online, his enthusiasm never seemed to decrease.

Most teachers ended their classes when the scheduled time expired. Dr. Bilal was different.

There were numerous occasions when students would remind him that the lecture time was already over. Instead of ending the session immediately, he would often create another Google Meet link and ask everyone to join again so that he could complete the remaining topics and answer questions.

From a student's perspective, it was surprising because there was no obvious benefit for him in extending the class. Yet he consistently invested additional time to ensure concepts were properly understood.

One memorable example occurred during Ramadan when a laboratory evaluation had to be conducted online. Dr. Bilal initially proposed holding the session shortly after Sehri, around 5:30 in the morning.

Students were asked to vote on the timing.

Most students preferred a later schedule, and the early-morning option was rejected. Eventually, the session was arranged before the university's official starting time, beginning at approximately 7:30 AM instead of 8:00 AM.

Experiences like these demonstrated how seriously he took teaching responsibilities. Looking back, I often felt that teaching was more than just a profession for him. His willingness to continue classes beyond scheduled timings suggested that he genuinely enjoyed helping students understand concepts thoroughly.

Even during online classes, when many people struggled to stay motivated, his energy remained consistent.

> Some teachers complete a course. Others genuinely invest themselves in their students' learning.

## Staying Connected with Friends

One of the things I missed most during online learning was the daily interaction with friends.

Before online classes, we studied together, discussed lectures face-to-face, and helped one another solve academic problems. Once everyone returned home, those interactions changed significantly.

Fortunately, technology allowed us to remain connected.

My close friends from Computer Engineering and I regularly communicated through WhatsApp. We often held group calls where we discussed lecture topics, upcoming quizzes, assignments, and preparation strategies.

These conversations became an important part of my routine.

Whenever one of us missed a lecture or failed to understand a concept, another student would usually explain it. Notes, screenshots, and important announcements were shared constantly.

Although online learning created physical distance, these discussions helped preserve some sense of teamwork and collaboration.

At the same time, students also discovered creative ways of coping with long online sessions.

It was common to see students attending lectures on laptops while simultaneously texting friends through their phones. Occasionally discussions drifted away from academics, especially during long classes.

Even so, those interactions helped maintain a feeling of normal university life during an unusual period.

## The Assignment That Created Panic

One of the most stressful moments of online learning occurred during a Calculus class.

On that particular day, our class representative was unable to distribute the lecture link on time. As a result, I joined the lecture approximately fifteen minutes late.

When I finally entered the meeting, I heard only the final part of the instructor's announcement.

We had fifteen minutes to complete and submit an assignment carrying significant marks.

At that moment, I did not even know what the assignment was.

The situation became even more frustrating because several other students had experienced the same problem. We quickly contacted classmates, attempted to understand the requirements, and rushed to complete the work before the deadline.

Although I managed to finish the assignment, another challenge appeared.

I did not know how to properly submit it through email.

Today that may sound simple, but at the time many students were unfamiliar with online submission systems. We had little experience attaching files, formatting emails correctly, and ensuring everything reached the instructor before the deadline.

By the time I figured out the process, my submission was slightly late.

The incident taught me that online learning required more than academic knowledge. It also demanded digital literacy and the ability to adapt quickly to unfamiliar systems.

## The "Six Crore" Incident

Despite the difficulties, online learning also produced some memorable and humorous moments.

One incident became particularly famous among my friends.

During an Object-Oriented Programming lecture, several students had stayed awake late into the night completing assignments. Exhaustion had become common because assignments, quizzes, and lectures often overlapped.

On one particular morning, I joined the lecture and lay down on a couch while listening. Unfortunately, I fell asleep.

Later, I discovered that I was not the only one.

Several of my friends had done exactly the same thing. We had all joined the lecture successfully, ensured our attendance was marked, and then unintentionally slept through most of the class.

Near the end of the lecture, the instructor asked a question related to a programming example involving a loop. Suddenly, one student loudly responded with the answer:

"Six crore!"

The unexpected response immediately woke me up.

Confused and disoriented, I looked at the screen and realized the lecture was almost over.

After class, I called my friends and discovered something hilarious. The same answer had woken them up as well.

All four of us had apparently slept through most of the lecture and regained consciousness at almost exactly the same moment.

Even today, that incident remains one of my funniest memories from online learning.

> Sometimes the most memorable university moments are not the planned ones.

## Looking Forward to Returning

Although online learning allowed the semester to continue, it was not an experience I wanted to extend indefinitely.

Long hours in front of a laptop screen began affecting my eyesight. My eyes frequently felt strained and tired after attending lectures throughout the day. Thankfully, the issue improved over time, but it highlighted one of the hidden costs of remote learning.

More importantly, I missed the university environment itself.

I missed meeting friends in person, discussing coursework after lectures, studying together before examinations, and simply experiencing campus life. Virtual communication helped, but it could never fully replace face-to-face interaction.

As rumors about reopening began circulating, many students became eager to return.

At first, however, the situation remained uncertain. The university had not yet provided detailed instructions regarding hostel access and arrival schedules. Since we had been away for a considerable period, I wanted to return slightly earlier to clean and organize my room.

Initially, that option was not available, which created additional uncertainty.

Eventually, an official notification was released, and plans for reopening became clear.

The announcement brought a sense of relief because it signaled the beginning of a return to normal academic life.

Looking back now, the online learning period taught me many valuable lessons. It improved my adaptability, strengthened my ability to learn independently, and introduced me to digital tools that later became essential in university education.

At the same time, it also reminded me how important classroom interaction, friendships, and campus experiences are to the learning process.

Although it was a challenging chapter of my university journey, it became an experience that helped me grow both academically and personally.
