## A Common Student Problem

One of the most interesting aspects of university life is that learning does not always happen through lectures, textbooks, or laboratory exercises. Sometimes the most valuable lessons emerge from ordinary situations that nobody initially considers educational.

During my second semester at UET Lahore Faisalabad Campus, one such lesson began with a problem that every student in the class could relate to: printing laboratory manuals.

For students, printing academic material is often more expensive than it appears. Beyond tuition fees, hostel expenses, transportation costs, and everyday living expenses, there are numerous hidden educational costs that accumulate throughout a semester.

One of those costs was laboratory manual printing.

Students in previous courses had spent approximately 2500 PKR each on printing manuals and related coursework. While that amount may not seem excessive on its own, it becomes significant when multiplied across multiple subjects and combined with other university expenses.

Apart from the financial burden, there was also the inconvenience.

Students frequently had to visit printing shops, wait for their documents, ensure formatting was correct, and make repeated trips whenever new submissions were required. As deadlines approached, printing shops often became crowded, making the process even more frustrating.

Naturally, students began discussing possible alternatives.

Since most manuals, assignments, and reports already existed in digital form, many of us believed that submitting soft copies would be more practical. Digital submissions would reduce costs, save time, and eliminate the need for constant printing.

Eventually, the discussion became so common that the entire class agreed to raise the issue with our instructor.

Our objective was simple.

We hoped to convince him to accept manuals in soft-copy format rather than requiring printed versions throughout the semester.

Most of us expected the conversation to end with a straightforward answer.

Either he would approve the request or reject it.

None of us expected what happened next.

## An Unexpected Response

When the topic was presented to Dr. Bilal Ahmad, he listened carefully to the concerns raised by students.

Everyone explained the same issues:

* Printing costs were high.
* Students spent considerable money on manuals.
* Printing shops were inconvenient.
* Digital files already existed.
* Soft-copy submissions would be easier.

The class was expecting a simple discussion about academic requirements.

Instead, Dr. Bilal Ahmad approached the situation from an entirely different perspective.

Rather than focusing solely on the problem, he began talking about opportunities.

At first, many of us were confused.

We were discussing how to avoid spending money on printing, while he was discussing how students could potentially earn money from the same situation.

His reasoning was surprisingly simple.

If hundreds of students across the university needed printing services, then clearly there was demand.

And where demand exists, opportunities exist.

Instead of viewing printing as a burden, students could view it as a business opportunity.

The conversation immediately became more interesting.

## Turning a Problem into an Opportunity

Dr. Bilal Ahmad suggested that a group of students could combine their resources and purchase a printer.

Rather than paying printing shops, students could create their own printing service for fellow students.

The idea was straightforward, practical, and surprisingly realistic.

What made the suggestion memorable was not merely the business concept itself, but the way he arrived at it.

The entire class had approached the issue from the perspective of reducing expenses.

He approached the same issue from the perspective of creating value.

Many of us were genuinely surprised.

There were around a dozen students participating in the discussion, and for a moment the classroom became unusually quiet.

The response was completely different from anything we had anticipated.

We expected a debate about submissions.

Instead, we found ourselves listening to an entrepreneurship lesson.

What impressed me even more was his willingness to support students who wanted to pursue such an idea.

During the discussion, he mentioned that if students were genuinely serious about developing the concept, he would be willing to support the initiative.

That statement caught everyone's attention.

It demonstrated that he was not merely sharing a theoretical business idea.

He genuinely believed that students should explore opportunities and think creatively about solving practical problems.

## Thinking Beyond Traditional Solutions

As the discussion continued, the idea became even more interesting.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad suggested that technology could further improve the service.

Rather than simply operating a traditional printing setup, students could create a small website where users could upload documents, place print requests, and collect their printed material later.

For Computer Engineering students, this immediately connected technology with entrepreneurship.

The conversation was no longer about printers alone.

It involved software, customer service, automation, business models, and problem-solving.

A simple complaint about printing costs had evolved into a discussion about building a technology-enabled service.

That moment stood out to me because it demonstrated an important principle:

> Innovation often begins when someone looks at an ordinary problem from an unusual perspective.

Most students see problems.

Innovative people often see opportunities hidden within those same problems.

## The Final Decision

Despite the interesting discussion, Dr. Bilal Ahmad ultimately remained convinced that maintaining laboratory manuals in printed form was important.

From an academic perspective, he wanted proper documentation and records of student work.

In the end, the requirement for hard-copy manuals remained.

However, what I appreciated was that he also considered the students' situation.

Rather than requiring printed submissions repeatedly throughout the semester, he agreed that students could continue submitting their work in soft-copy form during the course.

Only at the end of the semester would the manuals need to be printed and submitted.

This compromise significantly reduced inconvenience.

Students no longer had to visit printing shops again and again after every lab task.

Instead, all printing could be completed at once near the end of the semester.

Although our original request was not fully accepted, the final solution balanced academic requirements with student convenience.

It showed that he cared about maintaining standards while also understanding practical student concerns.

## Lessons Beyond Printing

Looking back, the most valuable lesson from that discussion was not actually about printing manuals.

It was about mindset.

Most of us entered the conversation focused entirely on a problem.

We wanted to reduce costs and avoid inconvenience.

Dr. Bilal Ahmad approached the same issue differently.

He immediately began searching for opportunities hidden within the problem.

That way of thinking is valuable not only in business but also in engineering and life.

Many successful products, services, and companies began because someone noticed a common frustration and decided to create a solution.

The ability to identify opportunities where others see obstacles is one of the foundations of innovation.

As Computer Engineering students, we spend much of our time learning technical concepts, programming languages, databases, and system design.

These technical skills are extremely important.

However, discussions like this reminded me that technical knowledge becomes even more powerful when combined with creativity, initiative, and entrepreneurial thinking.

A printer alone is just a machine.

A website alone is just software.

But when combined to solve a real-world problem, they become part of a meaningful solution.

That realization made the discussion memorable.

## A Memorable Classroom Moment

Years from now, I may not remember every assignment or every laboratory task from my second semester.

However, I will probably remember that classroom conversation.

What began as a complaint about printing expenses transformed into an unexpected lesson about innovation, entrepreneurship, and problem-solving.

The experience taught me that solutions are not always found by asking how to avoid a problem.

Sometimes the better question is:

"How can this problem become an opportunity?"

That simple shift in perspective completely changed the discussion and left a lasting impression on many students, including me.

Throughout my academic journey, I have encountered many lectures and technical concepts. Yet some of the most valuable lessons came from ordinary classroom discussions like this one.

As a Computer Engineering student documenting my experiences through this GitHub portfolio, I consider this moment worth preserving because it demonstrates an important principle that extends beyond any specific course:

Technical skills are valuable, but the ability to think differently, recognize opportunities, and create solutions is equally important.
