### A Common Student Problem

One of the most interesting aspects of university life is that learning does not always happen through lectures, textbooks, or laboratory exercises. Sometimes the most valuable lessons emerge from ordinary situations that nobody initially considers educational. During my second semester at UET Lahore Faisalabad Campus, one such lesson began with a problem that every student in the class could relate to: printing laboratory manuals.

For students, printing academic material is often more expensive than it appears. Beyond tuition fees, hostel expenses, transportation costs, and everyday living expenses, there are numerous hidden educational costs that accumulate throughout a semester. One of these was laboratory manual printing. Students in previous courses had spent approximately 2500 PKR each on printing manuals and related coursework. While this amount may not seem excessive on its own, it becomes significant when multiplied across multiple subjects and combined with other university expenses.

Apart from the financial burden, there was also inconvenience. Students frequently had to visit printing shops, wait for their documents, ensure correct formatting, and return multiple times whenever new submissions were required. As deadlines approached, printing shops became crowded, making the process even more frustrating. The time lost in this cycle was something that affected almost every student in the class, regardless of which subject they were printing for.

Because of this, students began discussing possible alternatives. Since most manuals and assignments already existed in digital form, many of us believed that soft-copy submissions would be more practical, cost-effective, and time-saving. Eventually, the entire class agreed to raise this concern with our instructor, expecting a simple approval or rejection. We came prepared with reasons and, looking back honestly, quite a few excuses as well.

### An Unexpected Response

When the issue was presented to Dr. Bilal Ahmad, he listened carefully to the students' concerns. We expected a straightforward academic response about submission rules and formatting requirements. Instead, he shifted the entire direction of the discussion.

Rather than focusing only on the problem, he began talking about opportunity. He explained that if hundreds of students require printing services, then there is clearly demand. And where demand exists, there is always potential for innovation and business. His suggestion surprised the entire class.

He proposed that a group of students could pool their resources, purchase a printer, and offer printing services to other students on campus. Instead of seeing printing as a financial burden, we could see it as a practical business opportunity. What made this even more memorable was how he responded to every concern the class raised. Whenever a student brought up a reason why the idea would not work, he had a calm and practical answer ready. It felt less like a lecture and more like watching someone dismantle every excuse one by one, in a way that was not dismissive but genuinely constructive.

He also mentioned that if students were serious about pursuing such an idea, he would be willing to support them. This showed that his suggestion was not theoretical but rooted in practical encouragement. As the discussion continued, he further developed the idea by introducing a technology component. He suggested that a simple website could be created where students upload their files, place a printing request, and receive a scheduled pickup time. When that time came, their documents would be ready for collection. This transformed a basic printer purchase into a structured, tech-enabled service model that could realistically operate on a university campus.

The reaction across the class was genuine surprise. Nobody had walked into that discussion expecting to leave thinking about websites, service models, and student entrepreneurship. We had come with a complaint, and we left with an idea.

What happened afterward was also interesting. A student from another section, Section A, actually went ahead and purchased a printer. In our own group, some students seriously considered taking up the idea as a side project, but it eventually did not move forward. Still, the fact that even one student acted on it showed that the idea was not just inspiring in the moment but practically believable.

### Lessons Beyond Printing

Although the final decision still required printed manuals, a compromise was reached. Dr. Bilal Ahmad made the call himself, without any formal announcement at the time. He quietly decided that students would not need to submit hard copies during the semester, and only informed us later. Soft-copy submissions would be accepted for ongoing work, with printed copies required only at the end of the semester.

I found out about this later than I should have. By the time the announcement reached me, I had already printed two or three of my manuals. It was one of those small frustrations that stays with you, not because of the money, but because of the timing. That experience also made the lesson more personal. The cost and inconvenience that the whole class had complained about had already landed on me before the solution even arrived.

Looking back, the most important lesson from this discussion was not about printing at all. It was about mindset. Most students see problems and try to avoid them. Dr. Bilal Ahmad demonstrated a different approach — he identified opportunity within the same problem. This ability to shift perspective is essential in engineering, entrepreneurship, and innovation.

As Computer Engineering students, we learn technical skills such as programming, databases, and system design. However, technical knowledge becomes far more powerful when combined with creativity and problem-solving thinking. A printer is just a machine, and a website is just software. But when combined with the right idea, they become a complete solution to a real-world problem. That realization made this classroom discussion one of the more memorable moments of my second semester.

This discussion took place during our Database Systems course, and it was Dr. Bilal Ahmad who taught that course. His academic background extends well beyond Database Systems into Artificial Intelligence, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning. The same problem-solving mindset he demonstrated in that classroom conversation reflects the thinking behind his applied computing research. It was a reminder that the skills we were building in Database Systems were not isolated academic exercises but part of a broader way of approaching real-world challenges.
