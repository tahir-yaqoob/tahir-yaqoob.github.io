# A Common Student Problem

One of the interesting things about university life is that learning does not always happen through textbooks, lectures, or laboratory exercises. Sometimes valuable lessons emerge from everyday problems that students face. During my second semester, one such lesson came from a discussion about something very ordinary: printing laboratory manuals.

Like many students, I was concerned about the cost and inconvenience of printing academic materials. In a previous course, students had spent approximately 2500 rupees each on printing manuals and related documents. While that amount may not seem enormous at first, it becomes significant when combined with tuition fees, hostel expenses, transportation costs, and other educational expenditures.

Printing also created practical difficulties. Students had to find printing shops, wait in queues, ensure that files were formatted correctly, and spend additional time managing paperwork. Whenever deadlines approached, printing shops became crowded, making the process even more frustrating.

As these concerns became more common, discussions began among students regarding whether there might be a better solution. Since most assignments and manuals already existed in digital form, many students felt that submitting soft copies would be more convenient and cost-effective.

Eventually, the entire class agreed that the issue should be discussed with our instructor. We hoped that permission might be granted to submit manuals electronically instead of printing large numbers of pages.

At that point, most of us expected a simple yes-or-no answer.

What happened next, however, turned an ordinary classroom discussion into an unexpected lesson about entrepreneurship and innovation.

# An Inspiring Perspective from Dr. Bilal Ahmad

When the topic was presented to Dr. Bilal Ahmad, he listened carefully to the concerns raised by students. Everyone explained the difficulties associated with printing manuals, including the financial cost and the inconvenience involved.

Instead of focusing only on the problem itself, Dr. Bilal Ahmad encouraged us to think differently.

Rather than viewing printing as a burden, he suggested viewing it as an opportunity.

His response immediately changed the direction of the discussion.

He explained that many students across the university face similar challenges. If printing services are expensive, inconvenient, or difficult to access, then there is clearly a demand for a better solution. Instead of simply complaining about the situation, students could potentially create a service that solves the problem while also generating income.

One suggestion he made was particularly memorable. He proposed that four or five students could pool their resources together, purchase a printer, and start offering printing services to other students on campus. By sharing costs and responsibilities, the investment would become manageable while creating a useful service for the university community.

The idea immediately caught the attention of many students because it transformed a routine academic issue into a potential business opportunity.

What impressed me even more was his willingness to support student initiatives. During the discussion, he mentioned that if a student genuinely wanted to pursue the idea but could not find partners, he would even be willing to join and support the initiative.

That statement surprised many of us.

It demonstrated that he was not merely presenting a theoretical business concept. He genuinely believed that students should explore practical opportunities and think creatively about solving real-world problems.

The discussion became even more interesting when he suggested incorporating technology into the solution. Rather than relying solely on traditional printing services, he proposed creating a simple website where students could upload files, place printing requests, and collect their documents later.

Suddenly, what began as a conversation about manual printing evolved into a discussion involving business models, customer service, technology, and entrepreneurship.

> The suggestion showed how technology and entrepreneurship can solve everyday student problems.

That moment stood out to me because it illustrated how innovation often begins with identifying a simple problem and then approaching it from a different perspective.

# Lessons Beyond the Classroom

As I reflected on that discussion later, I realized that the most valuable lesson was not actually about printing services. The real lesson was about mindset.

Most students, including me, initially approached the issue from the perspective of inconvenience. We focused on the difficulties we were facing and looked for a way to avoid them. Dr. Bilal Ahmad approached the same issue differently. Instead of seeing only a problem, he saw an opportunity.

That way of thinking is extremely valuable in engineering, business, and life in general.

Many successful products and companies began because someone noticed a common problem and decided to create a solution. Whether it is a mobile application, an online platform, or a technology startup, innovation often starts with identifying challenges that others overlook.

The classroom discussion also helped me appreciate the relationship between technology and entrepreneurship. As Computer Engineering students, we spend significant time learning technical concepts, programming, and system design. However, technical skills become even more powerful when combined with business thinking and problem-solving.

The idea of creating a website for managing print requests was particularly interesting because it demonstrated how software can be used to improve traditional services. Instead of manually collecting files and managing orders, a simple digital platform could streamline the entire process.

At the time, I was still developing my programming and database skills, but the discussion encouraged me to think about how technology could be applied outside classroom assignments. It made me realize that many opportunities exist for students who are willing to combine technical knowledge with creativity and initiative.

Another reason this experience remained memorable is that it reflected the broader teaching style of Dr. Bilal Ahmad. Throughout Database Systems, he frequently connected classroom topics to real-world applications. Rather than treating technology as an isolated academic subject, he often encouraged students to think about how technical skills can solve practical problems.

Looking back, the discussion lasted only a short time, but the idea stayed with me long afterward. It reminded me that opportunities are often hidden within ordinary situations. Problems that seem frustrating at first can sometimes become the foundation for innovative solutions.

As a Computer Engineering student building a GitHub portfolio to document my academic journey, I consider this classroom moment worth recording because it taught a lesson that extends beyond any specific course. Technical knowledge is important, but the ability to recognize opportunities, think creatively, and develop solutions is equally valuable.

What began as a conversation about expensive manuals became a lesson about entrepreneurship, innovation, and the power of looking at problems from a different perspective. For me, that simple classroom discussion became one of the most memorable experiences of my second semester.
