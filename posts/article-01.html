Searching for the Right Path: Universities, ECAT, and Early Confidence

Muhammad Tahir Yaqoob | Early admissions journey

The beginning

My journey toward becoming a computer engineer began with uncertainty but also curiosity. After completing my matriculation and FSc Pre-Engineering with strong marks above 85% overall, I started researching universities in Pakistan that offered reputable engineering programs. While browsing online, a few names appeared repeatedly: National University of Sciences and Technology, Pakistan Institute of Engineering and Applied Sciences, COMSATS University Islamabad, and especially University of Engineering and Technology Lahore.

Among these options, UET Lahore stood out because of its long history and reputation in engineering. Many of the other universities seemed financially difficult or complicated in terms of admission requirements. As a result, UET became my primary target.

Registering for ECAT

Admission to UET requires passing the Engineering College Admission Test, commonly known as ECAT. I registered for the test and paid the challan of about 2500 rupees. At the time, I thought of it almost like a gamble because ECAT had a reputation for being one of the toughest engineering entry tests in Pakistan.

Another challenge was timing. My board examinations were approaching, and the FSc syllabus had not been fully covered yet. Preparing for both the board exams and ECAT simultaneously was overwhelming. Because of that, I did not dedicate as much time to ECAT preparation as many other students did.

The first attempt

Still, I appeared for the first ECAT attempt with a few friends. Surprisingly, the test felt easier than expected. When the result came, I had scored around 230 out of 400. Considering my limited preparation, this seemed like a decent result.

Surprisingly, the test felt easier than expected, and my score made me feel that the path might be possible after all.

The overconfidence trap

Unfortunately, that small success made us overconfident. Instead of preparing seriously for the second ECAT attempt, we assumed the next test would be similar. Looking back now, that moment of complacency became one of the early lessons in my academic journey: sometimes a little success can make you underestimate the challenges ahead.

At that time, I still had not even decided which engineering field I wanted to pursue. I only knew one thing clearly: I wanted to become an engineer, and I wanted to study at UET Lahore.
