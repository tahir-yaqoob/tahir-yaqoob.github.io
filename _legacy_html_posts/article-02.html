Choosing Computer Engineering and the ECAT Reality Check

Muhammad Tahir Yaqoob | Choosing the field

After the first ECAT attempt, the next major step was submitting applications for specific engineering programs. Strangely, even after taking the test, I still had not decided which field I truly wanted to pursue.

At that point, I began researching different engineering disciplines online. Two fields appeared repeatedly in discussions about the future of technology: Computer Science and Computer Engineering. At first glance they seemed very similar, but the more I read, the more differences I discovered.

Guidance from a senior

To get clearer guidance, I contacted a senior student from UET Lahore who was studying Computer Engineering. Through WhatsApp, he explained the difference well. Computer Science focused more on software, algorithms, and programming, while Computer Engineering combined software with hardware, electronics, and computer systems.

Because I had always been curious about computer components and hardware, Computer Engineering felt like the right path for me.

Submitting preferences

So when the time came to submit my preferences, I targeted Computer Engineering at UET Lahore as my top priority. I also added Computer Science and Electrical Engineering as backup options. Based on previous years' merit lists, I believed my chances were strong.

The second attempt

However, the second ECAT attempt brought an unexpected challenge. Unlike the first test, the second ECAT was significantly harder. The questions were deeper and required more practice. Since I had not prepared seriously after my first attempt, the test felt much more difficult.

When the results came, my score dropped to around 217 out of 400. After combining my strong board results with the ECAT score, my final aggregate came out to about 74%.

While this was still respectable, it turned out to be slightly below the merit required for Computer Engineering at the Lahore main campus. Another mistake complicated things further: my program preferences were not arranged optimally, which prevented me from qualifying for some alternative seats.

For the first time, I realized that small decisions like preparation and preference ordering could significantly influence the final outcome.
