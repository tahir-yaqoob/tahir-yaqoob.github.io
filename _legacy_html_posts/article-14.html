Ramadan, Global Tensions, and the Shift to Online Classes

Muhammad Tahir Yaqoob | Adaptation during unusual circumstances

As the semester continued, the holy month of Ramadan began. Because of Ramadan, the pace of lectures became slightly slower so that students and teachers could manage their schedules more comfortably.

Around the time when we were preparing for our first quiz, an unexpected international situation occurred. A conflict between Iran and the United States created serious tensions in the region. As a result, the Strait of Hormuz was closed for some time.

This caused petroleum shortages and transportation issues in many areas. Because of these difficulties, the government shifted many activities, including education, toward online systems.

Returning home

Our university also announced that classes would be conducted online, and students were advised to return to their homes. Therefore, I left the hostel and traveled back home. The journey was not easy because online classes had already started, and while traveling by train I experienced internet connectivity issues and unfortunately missed two or three lectures.

After finally reaching home, I continued attending online classes regularly starting from March 19. Initially, the university announced that online classes would continue until March 31, but there was uncertainty about whether the period would be extended.

Although the situation was challenging, it showed how quickly education systems can adapt during unexpected circumstances.
